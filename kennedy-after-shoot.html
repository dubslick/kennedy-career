You did it

The shoot is done.
Now here's what happens next.

The photography session is the exciting part — but the work that happens in the days after is what actually gets Kennedy in front of casting directors. This guide walks you through every step from raw gallery to live submission profile.

⏳
Do Not Rush This
The most common mistake after a shoot is making final photo decisions while emotions are still running high. Wait at least one full night before reviewing proofs. You'll see the photos more clearly — and more like a casting director — with fresh eyes.
1
Get Your Files From the Photographer Do First
Before anything else, make sure you have everything you're owed. Galleries expire, links get lost, and photographers get busy. Download your files as soon as they're delivered.
  • Confirm you're receiving a gallery link (Pixieset, Shootproof, Google Drive, or similar)
  • Download all proofs immediately — don't wait, gallery links expire
  • Verify the files are high-resolution JPEGs — not compressed web previews or thumbnails
  • Confirm how many final edited selects are included in your package (typically 2–4)
  • Ask about turnaround time for retouched finals if not yet delivered
💾 Back up immediately: copy proofs to Google Drive or an external drive. Do not rely on the photographer's gallery link as your only copy after it expires.
2
Make Your Selects Most Important Step
This is where you narrow hundreds of proofs down to the 2–4 photos that will represent Kennedy for the next year or more. Take your time. Use this process:
  • First pass: Flag everything with potential — aim for 15–20 images without overthinking
  • Second pass: Narrow to your top 8–10 based on expression and energy alone. Ignore minor technical issues at this stage.
  • Third pass: Choose your final 2–4 using the selection criteria below
  • Get a second opinion from someone with industry experience — a drama coach, acting teacher, or trusted industry contact
  • Ask Kennedy which ones feel most like her — her instinct genuinely matters here
Ask yourself What you're looking for
Do her eyes have life in them? Non-negotiable. Eyes that are flat, tired, or disconnected are a hard pass regardless of everything else.
Does this look like her? Not posed, not performed. If you can see the effort, it's not the photo. Find the ones where she forgot the camera was there.
Does the expression match the look? Commercial = genuine warm smile. Theatrical = present and still. Personality = authentically herself.
Would you stop scrolling if you saw this? Gut check. Casting directors spend 2–3 seconds per photo. Does this one earn a click?
Is this the most flattering OR most castable? These aren't always the same. Parents pick flattering. Casting directors need castable. When in doubt, get that outside opinion.
🎯
What to Select for Each Lane
You want at minimum: one commercial look (big genuine smile, bright energy) and one theatrical look (eyes alive, no big smile, present and real). If the personality shot is strong, that's a valuable third. Only use the tween/YA look if it's genuinely competitive — a weak fourth photo doesn't help.
3
Request Your Retouching Send to Photographer
Once you've made your selects, send the file numbers back to the photographer for final editing. For an 11-year-old, the industry standard is minimal, natural retouching. Be explicit about what you want — and what you don't.
✓ Request These
  • Light blemish and spot reduction
  • Minor flyaway hair cleanup
  • Subtle brightness and contrast polish
  • Eye clarity and light enhancement
  • Color correction and white balance
  • Slight shadow reduction under eyes if needed
✕ Decline These
  • Skin smoothing or texture removal
  • Heavy teeth whitening
  • Feature alteration of any kind
  • Frequency separation or heavy airbrushing
  • Eye color changes or exaggeration
  • Body reshaping or slimming
📦 Ask for two versions of each final image: high-resolution (300 DPI) for print and physical submissions, and web-optimized (72 DPI, under 10MB) for casting platform uploads. Name convention: Kennedy_LastName_Commercial.jpg
4

5
Upload to Casting Platforms Go Live
This is where Kennedy becomes visible to casting directors. Your two priority platforms for the DC/Baltimore market are Actors Access and Casting Networks. Upload to both.
Actors Access
  • Go to actorsaccess.com → login → Photos
  • Upload primary headshot as your lead image
  • Add 2–3 alternate looks as secondary images
  • Label each photo by category (commercial / theatrical)
  • Best for: film, TV, streaming breakdowns
Casting Networks
  • Go to app.castingnetworks.com → login → Profile
  • Upload under the Photos section — same primary image strategy
  • Complete all physical fields (they filter by these)
  • Label each look category clearly
  • Best for: commercials, regional, print work
📸
Primary Photo Strategy
Use your strongest commercial look as the lead on both platforms — it casts the widest net. Casting directors scroll fast. Your lead photo has about 2 seconds to earn a click. Save theatrical and personality shots as secondary images — they expand Kennedy's range for casting directors who click through.
6
Pair With a Résumé Complete the Package
A headshot without a résumé is an incomplete submission. Every casting platform profile needs a résumé attached, and every physical submission prints it on the back of the headshot. If Kennedy doesn't have a formal résumé yet, now is the time.
  • Build the résumé using the Career Playbook template (separate document in this series)
  • Export as a PDF — name it Kennedy_LastName_Resume.pdf
  • Attach to both Actors Access and Casting Networks profiles
  • For physical submissions: print résumé trimmed to 8×10, stapled to the back of the headshot
  • A sparse résumé is completely fine at 11 — training and participation count. Never fabricate or exaggerate credits.
What goes on a child actor résumé at this stage: Name · Age range · Physical stats · Union status (Non-Union) · Any theater credits (school plays, community theater) · Training (acting classes, workshops) · Special skills (sports, instruments, accents, anything distinctive) · Parent contact info
