Representation

Finding Agents & Managers

DC/Baltimore is a real, active market — not LA, but there is consistent commercial, print, TV, and political/government-adjacent production work. These are the reputable agencies to research and submit to.

The DC/Baltimore market has a handful of legitimate, established talent agencies. An agent is not required to start submitting and auditioning — you can self-submit through the platforms above. But an agent expands access significantly, especially for union work and larger productions.

Agent vs. Manager

  • Agent — books jobs, takes 10% commission, regulated
  • Manager — develops career strategy, takes 15–20%, less regulated
  • Start with an agent. Add a manager later if warranted.

How to Submit to Agents

The Submission Package

Keep it simple, professional, and short. Agents receive dozens of submissions per week. Yours should be easy to review in 60 seconds.

01
Cover Letter (Email Body)
3–4 sentences max. Name, age, market, training summary, and one line about what you're looking for. Warm but professional. No superlatives ("most talented," "born to act"). Let the headshots speak.
02
Headshot Attachment
Attach your primary headshot as a JPEG (under 5MB). Name the file: Kennedy_LastName_Commercial.jpg. Do not embed images in the email body — they often break.
03
Résumé PDF
Attach as Kennedy_LastName_Resume.pdf. Keep it to one page. If you've already built the Actors Access profile, you can note "full profile at actorsaccess.com/Kennedy" at the bottom.
04
Follow-Up Strategy
If no response in 3–4 weeks, one polite follow-up is appropriate. After that, move on and resubmit in 6 months with updated photos if you have them. No more than that — persistence and pestering look the same.

Protect Kennedy

Scam Radar

The children's entertainment industry attracts predatory operators. Parents are the first and most important line of protection. Know these warning signs cold — and trust your gut when something feels off.

⛔ Immediate Red Flags — Walk Away
Upfront fees to be "represented" or "listed" — legitimate agents only earn commission when you book work
"Your child is perfect for modeling / commercials" cold approached at a mall or public place
Mandatory paid photography packages as a condition of representation
Guaranteed work, guaranteed callbacks, or "we have casting directors waiting"
High-pressure tactics — "we only have one spot left" or "sign today"
Requests to meet without a parent present, or for private communication with the child
Agents charging 15–20%+ commission — legitimate agents take 10%, period
No verifiable physical address, no agency website, or Google produces no credible trace
✅ How Legitimate Agencies Work
  • They review your submission and either pass or request a meeting
  • Meetings happen in a professional office setting with a parent present
  • They sign you (or don't) — no fees involved
  • They submit you to casting calls in their database
  • You audition. If you book, they take 10% of what you earn
  • You never pay them anything upfront
🔍 How to Verify an Agency
  • Search the agency name + "reviews" + "scam" before submitting
  • Check if they appear on SAG-AFTRA's franchised agency list
  • Search the Better Business Bureau (bbb.org)
  • Ask for references from current clients (legitimate agencies will provide)
  • Search the talent coordinator's name individually
  • Trust other parents in local theater / acting communities

Looking Ahead

Realistic Milestones

These are realistic, achievable milestones — not guarantees, but genuine markers of a healthy career trajectory. Use them as orientation points, not a scoreboard. Every actor's path is different.

Now
Month 1
Foundation Complete
Headshots selected and delivered. Résumé built. Actors Access and Casting Networks profiles live. First agent submissions sent. This is the hardest part — the rest builds on it.
Months
1–3
First Auditions
First auditions — self-tape or in-person — through platform submissions or background work. The goal here is not booking, it's experiencing the process and building comfort with auditioning. Every audition is a rep.
Months
3–6
Agent Interest or Signing
A response from one or more of the submitted agencies. This may be a meeting, a signing, or continued self-representation — all valid paths. An agent in this timeframe is great; not having one yet is still normal and manageable.
Months
4–9
First Booking
First paid or credited booking — student film, commercial, co-star, background with upgrade. Any booking validates the process and gives Kennedy a real set experience. This is the credit that starts the résumé.
Months
9–18
Demo Reel & Update
With a booking or two, Kennedy can assemble a brief demo reel — 60–90 seconds of her best on-camera work. Update headshots if appearance has changed significantly. The profile deepens and submissions get stronger.
Year
2+
Consistent Pipeline & Expanded Market
Regular auditions, a developing relationship with local CDs, and potential expansion toward NYC submissions. At this stage, if the momentum is real, evaluating a manager or dedicated NYC agent becomes a meaningful conversation.
📐 The Only Metric That Matters Right Now
Is Kennedy improving, enjoying it, and staying healthy? If yes — you're succeeding, regardless of what's booking. The bookings will come as a natural consequence of consistent training, professional materials, and intelligent submissions. Building a sustainable career takes 2–3 years of consistent effort. Anyone promising faster is selling something.
