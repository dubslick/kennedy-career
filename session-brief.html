Show casting directors exactly who Kennedy is — not a polished performance of a child actor, but her. Authentic, warm, present, and alive. A casting director should see these photos and want to meet her.

✅ Wardrobe Do's
  • Bring 5–6 top options for maximum flexibility on the day
  • Everything freshly laundered and steamed or ironed the night before
  • Clothes should fit her today — not too big, not too small
  • Layering pieces photograph beautifully (cardigans, light jackets, denim)
  • Bring a lint roller — it will be used
  • Simple accessories only — small earrings if she normally wears them

Presentation

Hair & Styling Notes

The guiding principle for an 11-year-old: keep it natural, keep it her. Casting directors should see what Kennedy looks like when she walks in the room. Over-styling is one of the most common — and most damaging — mistakes at this age.

⚠️ The Golden Rule

Casting directors for children actively screen out photos where kids look made-up or over-styled. At 11, this means essentially no makeup, and hair styled exactly how Kennedy normally wears it. Less is always more.

💇‍♀️
Hair
Wear it how she normally wears it. Freshly washed and styled, but not over-done. Natural texture and curls are an asset — don't flatten or over-straighten. Small flyaways are fine; perfection looks artificial.
💄
Makeup
Essentially none. Clear lip balm only. A light tinted moisturizer if needed for redness. Absolutely no foundation, eyeshadow, mascara, or contouring. If you can see it, it's too much.
✨
Skin Prep
Good sleep two nights before. Hydrated the day before. No new skincare products the week prior (risk of reaction). Blemishes can be minimized lightly in editing — don't stress them.

Direction

Expression & Personality Direction

Ask the photographer to capture multiple expression variations within each look. A good child headshot photographer will draw these out through conversation and genuine connection — not robotic posing direction. Request these six types:

😁
Full Genuine Smile
Her brightest natural smile. Commercial look. Coax it with something real — an inside joke, a funny video, her favorite thing.
🙂
Soft Closed-Mouth Smile
Warm and approachable without the big grin. Works across commercial and theatrical. Warm, not neutral.
👁️
Eyes Engaged, No Smile
The dramatic look. Still and present. Eyes that hold something. Think: the quiet before a big scene moment.
😂
Mid-Laugh Caught
Unguarded joy. Personality look. Happens organically — the photographer should be ready for these spontaneous moments.
😏
The Knowing Smirk
Playful, confident, a little cheeky. Great character look. Shows range and memorability beyond the "nice kid" lane.
🌿
Looking Off-Camera
Slightly angled gaze. Editorial feel. Versatile and sophisticated without trying too hard. Works well outdoors.
📸 Note for the Photographer

The first 20–30 minutes of a child's session are rarely the best shots. Allow warm-up time for Kennedy to settle in and connect with you before expecting the most natural expressions. If energy dips, take a break — walk around, have a snack, reset. The best photos come from genuine connection, not direction.

Environment

Background & Lighting Preferences

Industry standard has shifted toward natural-feeling images, even in studio settings. The goal is a background that supports Kennedy's look without competing with her.

Studio / Seamless
Best for theatrical submissions
  • Light grey or warm grey seamless paper — most versatile, widely accepted
  • Off-white works; pure white is too sterile for children
  • Avoid pure black — too dramatic, not appropriate for this age
  • Soft, diffused lighting — no harsh shadows
  • Catchlights in the eyes are essential — they bring life to the image
Environmental / Outdoor
Best for commercial and personality looks
  • Soft bokeh (blurred background) — greenery, warm brick, urban texture
  • Golden hour light is flattering and natural for kids
  • Overcast days provide beautiful, even diffused light
  • Avoid busy or distracting backgrounds — environment should be felt, not seen
  • Even one outdoor look adds variety and life to the package
🎯 Industry Standard Right Now

Today's market favors natural-feeling, lifestyle-adjacent images over formal studio setups. A light grey seamless background for theatrical, and at least one organic outdoor look for commercial/personality, is the ideal mix for a 2025 child actor package.

What Not to Do

Common Mistakes to Avoid

These are the most common — and most costly — mistakes parents and families make with child actor headshots. Being aware of them before the session will save you money, time, and submissions.

01
Over-Styling and Over-Posing
The child looks like a miniature adult, which makes casting directors uncomfortable and eliminates natural appeal. Kennedy should look like Kennedy — not a polished performance of what someone thinks a child actor looks like.
02
Chasing Someone Else's Look
Parents sometimes try to make their child look like a successful young actor they've seen. Casting directors see through this immediately, and it eliminates the most valuable thing Kennedy has: her uniqueness.
03
Not Allowing Warm-Up Time
The first 20–30 minutes of any child's shoot are rarely the best shots. Rushing into the session before Kennedy is settled and connected with the photographer will show in the photos.
04
Choosing the Photo You Love, Not the One That Works
Parents naturally gravitate to the most flattering photo. That's not always the most castable photo. Get a second opinion from someone with industry knowledge before final selection.
05
Over-Retouching
Light blemish reduction is fine. Smoothing skin texture, whitening teeth excessively, or altering features is not. Casting directors want to know what they're getting when Kennedy walks in the room.
06
Not Updating Photos Regularly
Children change quickly. Photos should be updated every 12–18 months, or whenever there's a significant appearance change. Using outdated headshots when Kennedy walks into a room is a serious credibility problem.
07
Treating It Like a Portrait Session, Not an Industry Tool
This is not a family photo or school picture. Every decision — wardrobe, expression, background, crop — should be made through the lens of what casting directors need to see. Beautiful and castable are not always the same thing.
